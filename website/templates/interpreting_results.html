Components of a Trading Strategy

While there are many variations of strategies that exist, all strategies need to have a few basic components. These are:

    • criteria for an entry
    • criteria to let you know when you're wrong on the trade
    • rules to let you know when to take profit

In practical terms these are your entry price, stop price (or risk price), and profit exit (or profit target). Every strategy tested here has rules for establishing all three of these components. Breaking this down further is the concept of R multiples. Since denominating P/L returns in raw dollar amounts can bring up some issues with commonality its best to record returns in units of R.

What is R?

R stands for risk multiples, or units of risk. R is found by taking the difference between the entry price and risk price. That price range is known as 1R, or 1 unit of risk. Once the 1R risk is established the profit or loss of that trade will then be denoted as a multiple of that 1R risk. The good thing about R multiples is that raw dollar amounts can be represented by units of R. For example if you want to risk $100 per trade then 1R would represent $100. If you were stopped out on the trade then you would lose 1R or $100. If you were to make a 2.5R profit on the trade then you would effectively make ($100 * 2.5) = $250. Some strategies here will have exit criteria that rely on the behavior of technical indicators, some will have specific profit targets denoted as multiples of R

It should also be noted that R risk is not the total amount put into a trade. R is simply represents what you stand to lose if your stop is hit.

Concept of R Multiples

How Risk is Measured and Established

While there are many methods to determine where to place a stop, for the purposes of this site, the risk price for all trades is found by utilizing a technical indicator called Average True Range (ATR). ATR is a measure that looks back in time x number of periods and provides a measure of volatility in terms of price increments. Higher readings mean more volatility, lower readings mean less volatility. For example, if the 14 period ATR for an asset with a price range between $10 and $15 might return a price of $0.75 meaning that average volatility in the last 14 periods was $0.75.

To determine a good place to put a stop you can take multiples of this measurement, which all strategies here do. So if the 14 period ATR is $0.75 you can multiply that by a factor of 1.5 to get $1.125. If an entry signal is generated you can then place your stop $1.125 below the entry price (if the trade is a long, for short trades the opposite applies)

Evaluation Metrics

In addition to requiring the basic components, its also important to understand how to evaluate the results of a trading strategy. Contrary to what may appear to be obvious, profitability isn't the end-all-be-all of strategy evaluation. If you are going to implement a strategy there are many factors that need to be considered in order to pick something that meets your personal requirements.

If your goal is profit over everything you are likely going to sacrifice consistency in individual trade returns and experience a very choppy equity curve.

If you goal is to take less risk and see less volatility in the equity curve its very likely your're going to end up leaving more money on the table.

If your goal is a smooth equity curve and maximized profits then you're all but guaranteed to do so at the expense of longevity. "Perfect" strategies are almost always very short lived.

There are many tradeoffs to consider. That being said, here is the list of evaluation metrics I use to assess the performance, strengths, and weaknesses of any particular permutation of a strategy you may find here.

Total Realized R

The final profit or loss of a strategy permutation for the given month as R multiples. If a strategy returns 20R in a month and your R risk per trade in real dollar terms is $100, then (20R * $100) = $2000.

Strike Rate

The ratio of winning trades to total trades, or the win rate.

Num Trades

The total number of trades that occurred within the backtesting period.

Avg Hold Time

The average number of seconds spent in a trade.

Avg Winner

Average R profit on winning trades.

Avg Loser

Average R loss on losing trades.

Expectancy

the average expected return per trade, denoted in R calculated as (Strike Rate * Avg Winner) - ((1 - Strike Rate) * Avg Loser). Strategies with a negative expectancy lose money on average and should be avoided

Largest Winner

The largest winning trade on record denoted in R. This should be compared to Total Realized R. If the largest winning trade is a significant portion of total profit then this tells you that the majority of the strategy's success for that period can be attributed one big trade. While this isn't necessarily a bad thing, if the strategy was only profitable because of one large winner it might not be as viable as it first appears.

Largest Winner

The largest losing trade denoted in R. Because the backtests are coded to work off of closing prices, this metric could very well be less than -1R as trades are exited on the first close that breaks below risk price (for longs) or above risk price (for shorts).

Max Drawdown

The largest number of consecutive losing trades. This can be compared to the total number of trades or examined individually and should also be taken into context with the granularity of data and the length of the trading period. A larger Max Drawdown means the strategy experienced a period where it was severely disconnected from the flow of the market and generated many consecutive wrong signals as a result. Although it could also mean stops were too tight. However there is a caveat. Smaller data granularity over larger timeframes will likely have periods of at least somewhat significant drawdown simply because the strategy has been exposed to a greater variety of market conditions.

Max Drawup

The largest number of consecutive winning trades. This can be compared to the total number of trades or examined individually and should also be taken into context with the granularity of data and the length of the trading period. A larger Max Drawup means the strategy experienced a period where it was very attuned to market conditions. This can be both good and bad. While having a drawup is nice at the time it could mean the strategy is/was overfit to market conditions at the time. If Max Drawup and Total Realized R are abnormally high (especially compared to more historical results) then this likely signals an overfit and you can reasonably expect Max Drawup, Strike Rate, Total Realized R or all three to drop in future periods.

MAE/MFE Concepts

MAE - Maximum Adverse Excursion. Calculated using high/low prices and is the farthest a trade moves against you over the lifetime of the trade

MFE - Maximum Favorable Excursion Calculated using high/low prices and is the farthest a trade moves in your favor over the lifetime of the trade

Winners Avg MAE

The average maximum adverse excursion (MAE) of all winning trades, denoted in R multiples.

Winners Std Dev MAE

The standard deviation of the maximum adverse excursion (MAE), denoted in R multiples, for all winning trades. Winners Std Dev MAE is useful because it offers some insight into the behavior of winning trades. For example, if the standard deviation of MAE for a string of winning trades is 0.2R and the average MAE is 0.4R, you add the standard deviation of 0.2R to 0.4R and get to 0.6R. Given a normal bell curve this represents 84% of the distribution of MAE, meaning that 84% of MAE values fall between 0R and 0.6R. This implies you can tighten your stops to from the originally planned 1R to 0.6R (which becomes the new 1R risk on future trades). Consequently, because your 1R stop is tighter this magnifies any gains as measured in multiples of R, meaning that you make more R multiples in profit given the same price movement.

Winners Avg MFE

The average maximum favorable excursion (MFE) of all winning trades, denoted in R multiples.

Winners Std Dev MFE

The standard deviation of maximum favorable excursion (MFE), denoted in R multiples, for all winning trades. For strategies that take their exit signals from technical readings. Winners Std Dev MFE can be compared to Average Winner to give you an idea of how much money you are leaving on the table. For example if the Avg Winner is 1.5R but the Winners Avg MFE is 6 and the Winners Std Dev MFE is 0.5R, this means your Avg Winner is very far outside the MFE bell curve distribution and there's a lot of room for improvement on profit exits. However for strategies with fixed profit targets, MFE will likely be meaningless.

Losers Avg MAE

The average maximum adverse excursion (MAE) of all losing trades, denoted in R multiples. In a perfect world this should always be 1R, however since loss exits are made on the first closing price to break the risk price, many of the strategies here will have a Losers Avg MAE greater than 1R.

Losers Std Dev MAE

The standard deviation of the maximum adverse excursion (MAE), denoted in R multiples, for all losing trades. This is useful as it gives insight into how much R you are losing past the allotted 1R amount (also known as stop slippage). Losers Std Dev MAE provides a metric to see how serious of a stop slippage problem exists in the strategy. The higher the number the more of an issue the strategy has with stop slippage. As previously mentioned, loss exits are made on closing prices that break risk price, not the risk price itself. In the real world you want a strategy that executes your order as close as possible to your stop when the time comes to get out of a losing trade. However that isn't always possible. Sometimes there's slippage, sometimes prices gap, sometimes trading gets halted, sometimes there's a wild print that throws execution algos off. All of this is difficult to accurately account for when coding a backtest.

Losers Avg MFE

The average maximum favorable excursion (MFE) of all losing trades, denoted in R multiples.

Losers Std Dev MFE

The standard deviation of maximum favorable excursion (MFE), denoted in R multiples, for all losing trades. Losers Std Dev MFE is useful as it offers some insight into the upside behavior of losing trades. Specifically, how far into profit a trade gets before it stops you out for a loss. For example, if the Losers Avg MFE is 1R and the Losers Std Dev MFE is 0.3R, this means that effectively 50% of your losing trades are falling between 1R and 1.9R before stopping you out. Obviously, this information is extremely useful as it suggest you can turn a significant portion of losers into winners by lowering the profit target to 1R (or tightening the exit parameters). While this is going to decrease the profitability of each individual trade, it will increase your strike rate and decrease the volatility of your equity curve.

Number of Bars

The number of total candles for the backtesting period.

Returns to Asset Corr

The correlation coefficient of the equity curve to asset candle data (1 = perfect correlation, 0 = no correlation, -1 = perfect inverse correlation). This basically tells you the degree the strategy returns follow the movements of the underlying asset. Generally speaking, its usually not good if the strategy returns are highly correlated to the asset its trading, especially if there are a large number of trades. Why risk the slippage and pay the commission on a strategy just to get the same returns as just holding the asset.

Equity Curve Regression Slope

The slope of the line of best fit of the equity curve. Basically this just provides the general direction of the equity curve. The larger the number, the more upward sloping the equity curve is. If the number is close to zero, there is no slope and the equity curve is generally flat. If the number is negative, this indicates a downward sloping equity curve (i.e. the strategy is losing money).

Equity Curve Regression Std Error

The average residual (also refered to as average error) of the regression line. This is calculated by averaging the distance between the regression line and the equity curve the regression line is fitted to. At a glance, this provides insight into how well the regression line describes the equity curve. Generally speaking, the higher the number, the choppier the equity curve will be. The lower the number, the smoother the equity curve is. When combined with the Equity Curve Regression Slope, you can come to a general conclusion about the general direction, and how choppy the equity curve is.

Kelly Criterion

Is a formula originally developed for bet sizing, but applies equally well for trading. Kelly Criterion tells you the position sizes you should be taking on your next trade. If the number is negative, you should avoid devoting any capital to the strategy. If its positive, this signifies the amount of your total capital that should be devoted to the next trade. For example, if Kelly Criterion reads 0.20 this means you can put 20% of your capital into the next trade. Basically, Kelly Criterion is a mathematical bet optimization formula that allows you take full advantage of winning streaks by increasing your bet size while negating your downside by cutting your bet size when you're on a losing streak.
